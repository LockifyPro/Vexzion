    Security best practices

    Ten habits that separate secure accounts from compromised ones.

    01

    Never reuse passwords

    A single breach can cascade across every account sharing that password. Each account deserves a unique credential.

    02

    Use a password manager

    Bitwarden, 1Password, and Dashlane let you maintain hundreds of unique passwords without memorizing a single one.

    03

    Enable two-factor authentication

    2FA ensures that even a leaked password cannot grant access without a second factor only you possess.

    04

    Longer passwords are exponentially stronger

    Moving from 8 to 16 characters doesn't double security — it raises the difficulty by trillions of iterations.

    05

    Never include personal information

    Names, birthdays, and pet names are the first entries in any dictionary attack. Avoid all predictable associations.

    06

    Monitor breach databases

    HaveIBeenPwned.com indexes billions of compromised credentials. Check your accounts regularly and rotate on hits.

    07

    Recognize phishing attempts

    Legitimate organizations never request credentials via email, SMS, or unsolicited links. Verify URLs before entering any password.

    08

    Segment your email identities

    Using a dedicated email for sensitive accounts limits exposure when lower-priority accounts are compromised.

    09

    Always log out on shared devices

    Session tokens can persist after browser close on some configurations. Always explicit logout on public machines.

    10

    Keep software updated

    Unpatched systems are the most common attack vector. OS and browser updates close vulnerabilities that no password can defend against.

    Frequently asked questions

    Everything you need to know about how PasswordAndLock works.

    Are my passwords stored anywhere?▼
    No. PasswordAndLock runs entirely within your browser. Nothing is transmitted to any server, logged, or persisted beyond your session. Closing the tab erases everything.
    How is randomness generated?▼
    We use window.crypto.getRandomValues() — the browser's cryptographically secure pseudorandom number generator (CSPRNG). This is the same engine browsers use for TLS, not the predictable Math.random().
    What is the difference between the four modes?▼
    Password generates a random character mix. PIN generates digits only. Memorable combines dictionary words into something pronounceable. Passphrase generates a word sequence separated by a character — high entropy with human readability.
    What password length should I use?▼
    16+ characters for standard accounts. 20+ for email, banking, and password manager master passwords. PINs follow the service's own constraints, typically 4–8 digits.
    What does the health checker evaluate?▼
    Eight criteria: minimum 8 characters, minimum 12 characters, uppercase presence, lowercase presence, digit presence, symbol presence, absence of common patterns (123, qwerty, password), and absence of character repetition (aaa).
    Can I edit a generated password?▼
    Yes. Click Edit to unlock the output field. The strength meter and health panel update in real time as you type, so you can see the impact of any change immediately.
    What is the Batch Generator?▼
    It generates 3, 5, or 10 passwords simultaneously using your current settings. Each result can be copied or loaded into the main field with one click.
    Is PasswordAndLock free?▼
    Completely free. No account, no subscription, no ads, no limits. Every feature is available to everyone.

    Common password mistakes

    The most dangerous habits people have — and how to fix them instantly.

    01

    Using the same password everywhere

    When one site is breached, attackers run automated "credential stuffing" attacks against hundreds of other platforms with your leaked credentials within hours.

    ✓ Fix: Generate a unique password for every account
    02

    Short passwords under 10 characters

    An 8-character password using common characters can be cracked in under an hour with modern hardware. Length is the single most impactful factor in password strength.

    ✓ Fix: Use 16+ characters minimum
    03

    Personal information in passwords

    Birthdates, pet names, favourite teams, and family names are all scraped from social media and fed into targeted dictionary attacks before generic brute-force even begins.

    ✓ Fix: Use fully random or passphrase-style passwords
    04

    Writing passwords on paper or in plain text files

    Physical notes can be photographed, and unencrypted text files are trivially read by malware. Neither offers any protection after initial access.

    ✓ Fix: Use an encrypted password manager (Bitwarden, 1Password)
    05

    Simple character substitutions (p@ssw0rd)

    Substituting @ for a and 0 for o has been in dictionary attack wordlists since the early 2000s. These patterns provide essentially zero additional security.

    ✓ Fix: Use genuinely random characters, not substitutions
    06

    Not enabling two-factor authentication

    A strong password alone is not enough. 2FA ensures that even a perfectly leaked password cannot grant access without the second factor in your possession.

    ✓ Fix: Enable TOTP-based 2FA on every critical account
    07

    Ignoring breach notifications

    Many users receive breach emails and ignore them for weeks. Attackers act within minutes of leaked credential databases appearing on dark web markets.

    ✓ Fix: Change affected passwords within 24 hours of any breach notification
    08

    Sharing passwords via SMS or email

    SMS and email are inherently unencrypted at multiple transit points. Any credential sent this way should be considered compromised and rotated immediately after use.

    ✓ Fix: Use a secure sharing feature in a password manager
